# Mobile Health Financial Context

## Purpose

This domain layer provides specialized financial intelligence for mobile healthcare, field-based clinical services, decentralized diagnostics, mobile phlebotomy, specialty specimen collection, clinical trials, corporate wellness, concierge healthcare, and related healthcare access models.

Mobile healthcare combines healthcare delivery, field-service logistics, clinical labor, transportation, geographic coverage, scheduling, specimen logistics, customer service, technology, and compliance.

Analyze it as both a healthcare organization and a distributed logistics network.

## The Five Economic Levels

### 1. Appointment Economics

Revenue may include base service fees, urgency premiums, mileage revenue, additional patients, additional kits, processing, shipping, and add-on services.

Direct costs may include clinical labor, contractor payment, travel time, mileage, parking, tolls, supplies, processing, centrifugation, packaging, shipping, merchant fees, and expected recollection cost.

Calculate appointment contribution margin, contribution margin percentage, revenue per clinical hour, and contribution per clinical hour.

### 2. Route Economics

Profitable appointments do not automatically create a profitable route.

Analyze appointments per route, total miles, drive time, clinical time, idle time, route revenue, route direct cost, route contribution, revenue per mile, contribution per mile, and revenue per total labor hour.

Route density is a primary financial variable.

### 3. Contract Economics

Analyze price per service, expected and guaranteed volume, geographic distribution, scheduling window, urgency, cancellation policy, recollection responsibility, payment terms, supplies, shipping, administrative workload, reporting, technology, and staffing requirements.

Calculate expected monthly revenue, expected monthly contribution, working-capital requirement, break-even volume, concentration risk, and operational burden.

### 4. Market Economics

Before entering a geography, analyze target customer concentration, existing demand, contract demand, contractor availability, labor cost, travel radius, geographic density, airport access, courier infrastructure, laboratory access, regulation, and management burden.

Model Beachhead, Density, and Scale stages.

Do not confuse geographic coverage with geographic profitability.

### 5. Platform Economics

Measure revenue and contribution per market, revenue per coordinator, appointments per coordinator, revenue per contractor, repeat customer rate, contracted recurring revenue, technology cost per appointment, administrative cost per appointment, geographic density, and customer concentration.

The objective is scalable healthcare access infrastructure.

## Complete Cost Stack

### Clinical
Clinician compensation, contractor fees, training, credentialing, and background checks.

### Mobility
Mileage, fuel, vehicle expense, parking, tolls, and travel time.

### Specimen
Collection supplies, processing, centrifugation, temperature control, packaging, shipping, and recollections.

### Coordination
Scheduling, dispatch, customer communication, client communication, documentation, and problem resolution.

### Technology
CRM, dispatch, communications, payment processing, automation, and AI systems.

### Compliance
Insurance, licensing, quality systems, policies, audits, and training.

Never calculate service profitability as only revenue minus clinician compensation.

## Decision Rule

Ask whether a decision improves appointment economics, route density, contract quality, market density, or platform scalability. A decision that improves none of these should be challenged.

## Strategic Principle

The strongest mobile healthcare business does not necessarily have the largest geographic footprint. It has the most economically productive network.
